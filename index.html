Teaching Philosophy

I believe that each student has the ability to understand and the desire to learn. They need an environment where they feel safe and comfortable. As a teacher, I believe that I am responsible for engaging my students and getting them involved in their own education. It is important to respect the differences of each student; cultural, racial, and each student’s learning style. Students have to be given space to discover and develop their own ideas.

John Dewey states “the teacher is engaged, not simply in the training of individuals, but in the formation of proper social life.” (1897, p. 80). At the end of a student’s education they are supposed to be equipped with all the skills and knowledge they need to be successful. They not only need have information to help them succeed in a career, but also know how to interact with others, handle stress, and how to analyze what they observe everyday. Students will watch television and accept that the world is the way the actors, and story portray. They never question what they see, read, or hear. Students need to be encouraged to question what they are taught; they need to be encouraged to seek explanations. They need to be instructed on how to raise these challenges while still being respectful of others and listen to differing opinions on a topic.

Success of a student will also come from identifying interests and using them to make education relatable. A teacher can help a student develop an interest in their education if the topic can be related directly to their lives. Getting a student interested is only half of what a teacher has to do to help them learn. A teacher needs to identify how each student learns best and how the class learns together. Teachers need to be able to base their activities on students and their learning skills.

I believe that a teacher has to be flexible with their activities and teaching style. If a teacher gets too comfortable in their way of presentation, or thinks they know the best way to learn their subject, the students may suffer. For example, if a history teacher believes that the best way to learn history is by reading the text book, copying the text, and copying notes from a presentation and that is the only way they teach; the students may be bored and not pay full attention. Teachers need to make their subjects exciting and have their students participate if they want them to learn.

When students participate in activities they can have “ah-ha” moments; moments of discovery and clarity. Interactions with their peers will allow them to explain an idea or have one explained from a perspective other than the teacher’s. If a teacher does not allow the students to discuss and discover they will not remember the subject after the test.

I believe that a teacher needs to be flexible, patient, understanding, open-minded and, most importantly, unbiased. I believe that a classroom needs to be an environment where judgement does not take place and the students can voice their ideas or frustrations. I believe that students need to be educated in both academics and social skills. It is the job of the teacher to respect their students’ differences and similarities and to help them grow and improve. A teacher also has to be constantly improving themselves by learning new theories and new ways to help their students. Being a teacher means constantly improving yourself and your students so that everyone can succeed in reaching their full potential.

References

Differences in the Classroom

As a teaching candidate at Northeastern Illinois University I have been introduced to many issues that change the learning environment such as political agendas or cultural clashes. Each student has a unique background, their own way of learning, and their own bank of prior knowledge. The university has a list of six proficiencies that I will address and demonstrate my progress with each proficiency.

When students enter a classroom, they bring their cultural beliefs, races, and traditions with them. Each culture has different values and emphasizes different traits in an individual. For example, in eastern Asia group betterment is valued and individualism is frowned upon where in the United States we encourage individual merit and reward it. Being aware of these differences between the students and incorporating the student cultures will give all students a chance to learn about a different perspective from their own. I will help my students to understand that everyone is allowed to have their own opinion and that they should not be discriminated against because of their beliefs, race, or ethnicity. The first artifact in my portfolio contains the pages of a website I helped create with a group of classmates. It is about a book called Holler if You Hear Me by Gregory Michie that discusses incorporating students’ cultures and backgrounds into the classroom and making learning meaningful.

During my own course work I have had the unique experience of making friends with classmates that come from communities very different from my own. I am from the suburbs where we had everything we needed, the classrooms were well stocked, and I had many options in terms of the courses I was allowed to take. I got to discuss their experiences from going to schools in the city. Some of them were no where near as fortunate as I was. Their experiences help me realize exactly the kind of environment I would be entering for my observations and student teaching. They also shared stories about their communities, the same cultures that my students may be coming from, to help me understand the students better.

The same artifact about "Holler if you Hear Me" can be used to demonstrate the second proficiency which states that “The candidate is aware and addresses local, national, and global events, and promotes agency for social justice and equity.” The proficiency encourages educators to make students think critically about material, ask how the information is relevant to them, and question the perspective the information is presented from. Does the perspective of the author align with the majority of public opinion? What are the thoughts of the people who do not agree, and why do they think the way they do? These are all important questions that have to be posed to students and that they have to keep in mind whenever they read text, watch television, or interact with any kind of media. Students constantly interact with media outlets that are sending them messages and shaping their thoughts so they have to be aware of these messages. If they do not agree with them, then they need the knowledge of how to create an educated differing opinion.

Every student has a preference for how they like to learn; usually this preference is the way they learn best. Some can read from a text book and understand the information while others need charts, graphs, and demonstrations to really understand what is happening. To encourage learning for all individuals an educator has to use many activities and teaching styles to get all students involved and make them feel involved in the classroom. I plan to incorporate the traditional lecture and discussion format for many of my classes; however, I also plan to assign projects, labs, and some discovery learning days to help all students enjoy being in my classroom. For example, to help the students that learn visually, I plan to use online videos, WebQuests, and interactive, online, dissections. Artifact two is a lesson prepared using multiple strategies to incorporate as many learning styles as possible. Every lesson cannot include an activity that uses the strength of each individual, however, a variety of activities should be used daily; I should change each day to use as many different types of activities as possible and keep all students involved.

It is also important to use many sources of text besides the text book. Some students will not have the skills necessary to comprehend the language of the text book assigned to their grade level while others will find the text book too easy. Either type of student will become disengaged with the text book quickly, requiring an educator to find other texts that teach the same material but at a level that is appropriate to the student’s abilities. These texts do not have to be text books, but can be articles or trade books that discuss the same topic being covered in class. Artifact three is a unit plan designed to incorporate many reading and comprehension strategies along with alternative texts for the unit. As the students build confidence in their reading skills, they participate more. If I want them to continue contributing to the learning experience I have to create a safe environment.

The first step in developing a respectful learning community is clearly stating classroom procedures and ensuring the students understand, and agree to, each procedure. They must also understand the consequences that would occur if they do not follow the procedures. This process will go more smoothly if the students are involved in deciding what procedures should be in place in the classroom. If the students are involved in creating classroom procedures, then they are more likely to follow the procedures without argument because they feel a sense of responsibility to uphold the standards they set for themselves. This respect for the classroom will translate to respect for each other because they worked together to create a fair environment. Having them taking part in the process will also make them think about another’s right to learn in a safe and fair environment. The respect for a classroom environment and community also applies to the communities they live in and the procedures society expects to follow. When the students respect the communities they live in, the community will be more willing to help them when they need it, for example, if students are attempting to raise funds for prom or a community service project, the members of the community are more willing to help if the students show respect.

The fifth proficiency states that “The candidate effectively utilizes concepts, issues, events, and themes from the perspective of diverse ethnic and cultural groups for continual improvement to make schools or clinical settings responsive to the needs of diverse students.” As I said earlier, different cultures prioritize different issues and characteristics. An issue that arises in a community that may not be important on a societal scale may be extremely important to a community. An educator has to be involved in the community they work in and be aware of the events that are important to the community. Whether an event matters locally or globally, it is going to affect the student’s emotions and their ability to learn. Being aware of all the major issues and events in the community will help a teacher prepare for the consequences of the event. That may mean that an educator has to postpone their plans to allow students time to cope, mourn, or whatever else may be necessary. Events that happen in the communities can also be use as teachable moments and help connect the material being taught to the student’s lives, making the learning more memorable.

I have known for a long time that I wanted to be a teacher, however, I did not decide until high school that I wanted to teach secondary education. My observation hours were completed in the Lincoln Park neighborhood at Latin School of Chicago in Chicago, Illinois. It is a private school that houses a lower, middle, and upper school. This year the school had started the process of reversing the order of science classes; instead of freshman taking biology first they are taking physics then chemistry and they will take biology as juniors and seniors.

The student I interviewed is a senior in high school in the Northwest suburbs. I have known her for about 10 years; when we first met she was a little shy, but as we saw each other more often she became more outgoing.

Part Four: Analysis

How do students construct knowledge, acquire skills, and develop habits of mind?

The science department at Latin School of Chicago began a process this year that would help their students better understand the sciences. The science department started freshmen on a new science track that was different from previous years because they would begin in physics instead of biology. Switching the order of progression would relate the science class to the math level the students could understand. As freshman, the students are enrolled in, or have taken, algebra that they can then apply to physics. Calculus is much easier to use in physics, however, most high school students will not take a calculus class until college. It is a much better option than waiting to teach physics until junior year when the students are taking advanced algebra with trigonometry or pre-calculus and have forgotten basic algebra. The students begin to use the information they are learning and incorporate it into information they have learned in the past. The students were building schemas and were able to come to the next class and relate the old information to the new material. They fit into Piaget’s formal operational stage where they are expected to form hypotheses about what is going to happen based on past knowledge (Santrock, 2011, p. 57).

The students take part in labs two days a week, allowing them to apply what they have been taught during lecture and discovering how the theories apply to real situations. It is similar to allowing children to play and discover what the world is about; the main difference was that the students in the lab are directed towards a certain discovery.

In some of the classes the students spent time practicing on a worksheet with the teacher and were allowed to ask questions as needed. The repetition helped the students to remember the formula they were required to know and as they became closer to completing the worksheet they became faster at the calculations.

Each student will have their own habits formed when they enter high school. A teacher has to help their students think about the information they are learning and why it is important. The teacher can repeat themselves and ask the students to memorize facts to write on the test, but the students may not remember the information later. Students need to build knowledge through understanding not memorization.

How do students’ physical, social, emotional, ethical, and cognitive development influence learning?

When students enter high school some may be well into puberty, some just starting, and some still waiting to start puberty. According to Santrock (2011), adolescents who begin puberty earlier have higher self esteem and those who start late have a lower self esteem (p. 88). This self esteem level will help determine whether the student develops problems; behavioral or mental. Santrock (2011) also states that the actions the student takes can change their brain development (p.89). These changes can affect their cognitive development and their ability to construct knowledge into useful schemas.

If a student is not as socially developed as their peers, they may be avoided and have a difficult time making friends since others will see them as immature. These students may also have issues working in groups because they may not know how to communicate their ideas to their group members.

The student that is emotionally immature may not have a hard time finding friends, but they may have other behavioral problems that cause them to be disruptive. This kind of student may have outbursts in class, disrupting everyone, or become completely unfocused if they do not understand the material causing them to fall farther behind.

A teacher is responsible to their students to identify if any student is severely behind in the development of their physical, social, emotional, ethical, or cognitive abilities. They have to help the student in what ways they can before asking for additional help. A teacher has to be aware that if a student is acting inappropriately, or performing poorly, that there are underlying causes. If the cause is that the student does not understand the material, then the teacher must set aside some time to help that student and keep them from falling farther behind.

What are some of the different approaches to learning and performance that you have seen? What do you recommend to deal with different learning styles, multiple intelligences, and performance modes?

Many of the classrooms used the same approaches to teaching. The most common aspects were lecturing, worksheets, and review of past assignments. The order varied by teacher. During lectures the students were expected to be taking notes and asking questions immediately if they did not understand an idea. The teachers always had a visual, whether it was a power point presentation with the notes on the slides, or writing the notes on the white board for the students to copy down.

The worksheets included verbal explanations of the instructions, what was expected while working on the assignment, and how long they would have to complete the activity. The teachers were available for questions or if a student needed further clarification. In one of the chemistry classes, the majority of the students were struggling with one particular problem so the teacher pulled the class back together to solve the problem as a whole. He asked simple questions that related the past material to the worksheet so the students could understand the connection and how the information built on itself.

Usually the worksheets were to be done individually, however, the teachers encouraged the students to work with classmates on problems they did not understand. They wanted the students to do what they could and not have one person do all the work while the others copy. During the AP Environment class the students each had a set of data they were responsible for in their group. The group would then discuss why the calculations were correct or if there was a mistake before comparing their answers with the other groups in the class.

When questions were taken on the homework, every teacher took the most common question the students had issues with and explained the problem. Usually, by the time homework was being reviewed time was short. If a student had other problems they wished to discuss the student and teacher planned a time to meet to finish clarifying any issues.

One feature of teaching was common among all the teachers; they were all accessible to their students and approachable. The students did not seemed intimidated to go ask for more help, and were not ashamed if they had a question. Not everyone learns the same way and these teachers were aware. Their explanations of a theory were never worded the same way twice. They also included a range of activities: worksheets, lectures, readings, group projects, independent study, labs, and videos to make the subjects relate to all the students.

Howard Gardner stated that there are eight frames of mind: verbal skills, mathematical skills, spatial skills, bodily-kinesthetic skills, musical skills, interpersonal skills, intrapersonal skills, and naturalist skills (Santrock, 2011, p. 188). A student can excel in one or many of these frames of mind. A teacher needs to identify how each student learns best and how the class learns together. They then need to base their activities to fit the students learning needs. The sciences are closely related to math but if the class is full of students that are bad with numbers, then worksheets filled with numbers may help the students build their mathematical skills.

How do human development, learning theory, neural science, and the ranges of individual variation within each domain interact?

As a human develops and grows myelination occurs in the brain, allowing for information processing to happen faster (Santrock, 2011, p. 93). This is important in physical development because as a human grows they need to develop greater muscle control and motor skills. The neurons have to be able to send sensory information to the brain quickly if the student is to learn. The faster the brain can process information, the more complex and abstract ideas the student can understand and build into their learning schemas.

As a student goes through school they should be able to understand more complicated subjects like Calculus and Chemistry. If a student has a defect in the myelination process they will likely be cognitively underdeveloped for their age since information processing does not occur as quickly. In adolescents, it is important that the corpus callosum develops because it assists with information processing (Santrock, 2011, p. 95). Other parts of the brain have to develop as a student ages such as the amygdala, which controls emotions, so the student can develop skills to control their emotions and display behaviors that are socially acceptable.

Teachers have to be patient when dealing with students that may be late developing. They may need extra time on assignments and extra help when it can be offered. Alternative assignments can also be used to help these students understand better if methods like lecturing and worksheets are not sufficient.

If the student is having behavioral problems it has to be explained to them why the behavior is inappropriate and they need help realizing an appropriate way to display their emotions. Performing behaviors that are expected of them will help the brain develop and the student can begin to catch up with their classmates.

How do cultural and gender differences affect communication in the classroom?

Respecting cultural differences is extremely important when dealing with students. Behaviors that are expected in one culture may not be as heavily emphasized in another. Words, phrases, and even gestures may have a different meaning to someone from a different culture. For example, what an American would call a “peace” sign means “up yours” in Australia. According to Ellen Johnson (1997), differences in cultural norms may cause communication issues, for example, some students may feel shame if they are wrong in public, so unless they know the answer, they will not answer a question when called (p. 48). The lack of an answer may cause the teacher to believe that they do not understand. Many cultures teach their students it is better to be part of a group than an individual; the student from this kind of culture may avoid showing their intelligence by answering a question because they would put themselves above the group.

Today, gender differences do not have as much of an affect on communication in the classroom because we have mostly defeated the idea that men are the breadwinners of the home and women are the caretakers. Caroline Hunt developed an idea called “municipal housekeeping” meaning that they could manage the house by buying goods instead of making them, and take classes to become educated and partake in social reform (Spring, 2011, p. 207).

An issue that may be caused by gender differences is some students not being comfortable in the same class with the opposite sex, making them shy and unwilling to participate (Johnson, 1997, p. 50).

A teacher has to meet their students early and understand their backgrounds so break downs in communication do not get misconstrued as the student not understanding. It may require the teacher to read about the student’s culture or meet with the student and their parents to better understand how to make the student the most comfortable in the classroom. To help students from other cultures get involved a teacher should vary the ways they present information and the activities the students are assigned.

Part Five: Conclusion

What do you conclude from the interview?

I think the girl I interviewed is a typical suburban high school student. She may have experienced some discrimination when she was younger and it either did not bother her or she has moved on. How will you use this information in your teaching?

I learned a lot from observing the teachers and students at Latin School of Chicago. I saw the differences in the way the Freshman and Seniors were treated by the teachers and how the students reacted to being treated as equals instead of students. The Freshman had rules and a structure they were expected to follow because they could not handle the freedom of the discussion the Seniors had in some of their classes. In one Senior class they politely debated the topic of the day versus in a freshman class, where whenever the teacher was not talking there were many side conversations.

I saw effective and ineffective ways of getting a class back on track when the students went on tangents. One teacher became stern and said they had to finish the topic and they could talk about other things later. Another teacher allowed the conversation to go on for a short time then tried to get the students focused, which they could not do and ran out of time in the class.

Finally, I learned what activities the teachers have found the most effective assignments. Every teacher allowed the students some time in class to look at the homework assignment for the night and ask questions before they left for the day. The students seemed to like the chance to get clarification before they were expected to do the assignment on their own. When answering questions, the teachers never just gave the answers. They asked the students what they thought was correct and if they were not, the teacher would ask simpler leading questions to guide the student instead of just correcting the mistake.

What are your recommendations for teachers?

My first recommendation to teachers is to be flexible. The students can learn and they want to, but sometimes they do not know how to best use the information presented to them. It is the teachers responsibility to help the students develop the skills they need to organize their thoughts and apply their prior knowledge to what they are currently learning. If the students are not understanding the information, then the teacher has to change the way the information is being presented or explained so the students understand. It also helps to change the presentation of the material into something that relates to the students’ everyday life. For example, if the students are not grasping the function a ribosome has in a cell by written explanation alone, a video could be used to show them how the process occurs.

Second, a teacher has to be open minded. They have to be willing to learn about their students if they are going to teach them properly. Each student is different and a teacher is responsible for learning as much about each student as possible. If a teacher focuses on the way just a few students learn the best, the rest of the class will not be successful. When all the homework assignments require copying text from a book, many of the students will do the work but not understand the text or think about what the text says. Incorporating worksheets with fill-in-the-blanks will make the students read the book and not just write the words.

Lastly, I would tell anyone who wants to teach to listen. The students will provide opinions when given the chance. If you continue to teach new information when your students are telling you they do not understand the old material, you are setting them up for failure. If there is a project that they did not enjoy and you continue to assign the same kind of project, they will not put forth the effort you would expect. Listening to the students and learning what they do and do not like is especially important for first time teachers.

Artifact 11: Impact on Student Learning

Introduction

Learning how to properly construct a reference list in American Physiological Association (APA) format is essential for scientific writing. Students need to learn and practice this skill. Another skill students need to learn is sorting through sources and determining which are reliable and which are not. The purpose of the lesson was to determine the effectiveness of a short 15 minute discussion. The class was a junior level International Baccalaureate Biology class at Amundsen High School. There were 20 students in the class.

Methods

The students were give a seven question survey at the beginning of class. They were told verbally and in writing that the questions would not be graded and that guessing was not penalized. There was three multiple choice questions and four true or false questions. A survey was given at the end of the lesson with the same questions but in a different order. Again the students were told it would not be graded. To compare the data, the questions on the post-lesson survey were analyzed in the same order as the questions on the pre-lesson survey.

Data

Pre-Lesson: Before the lesson, the students were given a seven question survey and as much time as they needed to finish. All the students were done within five minutes. Table 1 shows the survey score before the discussion. Most of the students, 16, received a 8 or higher out of 11 on the pre-lesson survey.

Table 2 shows which questions the students go wrong. Question 1 asked the students “ What format do you use for science papers?” and they were asked to choose between MLA or APA. Question 3 asked the students to choose what kind of websites are the best for research. They were asked to choose “all the apply”. Question 5 asked about capitalization of the title in a reference listing.

Post-Lesson: After the discussion the students were given as much time as needed to answer all the questions on the survey. Most students took about three minutes. Table 3 shows the distribution of scores after the lesson. All of the students scored at least 8 out of 11, and 13 scored 10 or 11 out of 11.

Table 4 shows which questions were incorrect the most frequently. Question six was a true or false question that stated if not all information was available for a particular reference, then you do not include it in your bibliography. Question 3 was frequently incorrect on the post-lesson survey. No more than eight students got any single question wrong.

Table 5 shows the difference in the average scores between the pre and post-lesson surveys. The average score in the Pre-lesson survey was 7.9 points and the average on the post-lesson survey was 9.74 points.

Discussion

The data shows that the students had some prior knowledge about the topic of citation and research. Based on questions asked during the discussion I discovered that one of the concepts, wikipedia is not a source itself but can lead them to good sources, had been covered in a previous class. I also overheard students make the comment “MLA is the same as Chicago Style right?” It was not a topic I had anticipated covering in the lesson but the misconception had to be corrected. I do not believe that APA format was not a topic they had ever been introduced to since almost every student had question 1 incorrect on the pre-lesson survey. On the post-lesson survey it is evident that the students had a good understanding that APA is used in all scientific writings since for question 1 only one student marked the wrong answer. Another question with a large improvement was question 5 that asked about a rule specific to APA format. I expected this result since the students had never been exposed to scientific writing before.

Students were stumped by question 3 throughout the lesson. It asked them to choose which websites were the best to use for research and some still choose .com and .net as good options. This is concerning since it was explicitly covered during the discussion. It could have been that the lesson was too fast and they did not understand why those were not reliable sources. The only question more students were wrong more often on the post-lesson survey was question 6. I did not explicitly say you have to cite the source not matter how little information you could find. An improvement on the lesson would be to teach the students methods for finding the information if it not obvious.

If I were to teach the lesson again I would like to have the students work with a partner or complete some example citations. I would also place this lesson with a research based project so the students could apply the lesson immediately. When this lesson was taught the class was designing experiments and if they were using someone else’s design, then it should be cited. I would also like to use this lesson to impart on the students the importance of science being based on evidence and that evidence can come from experiments you perform yourself or from someone else’s experiment. However, if they use someone else’s data it must be cited. I was only able to touch on the subject during this lesson.
